📝 German Articles & Cases

Master the foundation of German grammar: definite, indefinite, and possessive articles across all cases with comprehensive examples and clear rules.

📖 Types of German Articles

1. Bestimmter Artikel (Definite Article) - "THE"
Used when referring to specific, known things. Equivalent to "the" in English.
Examples:
Der Hund spielt. (The dog plays) - specific dog we know
Die Schule ist groß. (The school is big) - specific school
Das Auto ist rot. (The car is red) - specific car
2. Unbestimmter Artikel (Indefinite Article) - "A/AN"
Used when referring to non-specific things or introducing something for the first time. Equivalent to "a/an" in English.
Examples:
Ein Hund spielt. (A dog plays) - any dog, not specific
Eine Schule ist groß. (A school is big) - any school
Ein Auto ist rot. (A car is red) - any car
3. Negationsartikel (Negative Article) - "NO/NOT A"
Used to negate nouns, meaning "no" or "not a/an". Similar to indefinite article but negative.
Examples:
Kein Hund spielt. (No dog plays) - no dog at all
Keine Schule ist groß. (No school is big) - no school
Kein Auto ist rot. (No car is red) - no car
💡 Key Understanding:
Definite vs Indefinite: Think of "Humayun's Tomb" vs "a tomb"
• Humayun's Tomb = Das Humayun-Mausoleum (specific, famous tomb we all know)
• A tomb = Ein Mausoleum (any tomb, not specific)
• No tomb = Kein Mausoleum (negating the existence)

🔄 German Cases Explained

Nominativ (Nominative Case) - THE SUBJECT
The subject of the sentence - who or what is performing the action.
How to identify:
Ask "Wer oder was?" (Who or what?) does the action
Der Mann liest. → Wer liest? Der Mann.
Akkusativ (Accusative Case) - THE DIRECT OBJECT
The direct object - who or what receives the action directly.
How to identify:
Ask "Wen oder was?" (Whom or what?) receives the action
Ich sehe den Mann. → Wen sehe ich? Den Mann.

⚠️ Critical Rule:
Only masculine articles change in Akkusativ!
• der → den (definite)
• ein → einen (indefinite)
• kein → keinen (negative)
• Feminine, neuter, and plural stay the same!
🚨 Critical Rule: Subject NEVER Changes Case!
Common Beginner Mistake: Thinking the subject changes when an object is added
❌ Wrong thinking:
"Der Hund spielt" → "Der Hund spielt Fußball" → "Den Hund spielt Fußball"
✅ Correct:
Der Hund spielt. (subject stays Nominativ)
Der Hund spielt mit den Fußball. (subject still Nominativ, object becomes Akkusativ)
(The dog plays with the football - specific ball object)
Remember: The subject is ALWAYS the "doer" and stays in Nominativ. Only the direct object (what receives the action) becomes Akkusativ.
Memory trick: "The dog always remains 'der Hund' no matter what it does - it's what the dog does TO that changes!"

🎯 Case Examples in Context

Nominativ Examples

  • Der Hund spielt. (The dog plays)
  • Eine Katze schläft. (A cat sleeps)
  • Das Kind läuft. (The child runs)
  • Keine Autos fahren. (No cars drive)

Akkusativ Examples

  • Ich sehe den Hund. (I see the dog)
  • Er kauft eine Katze. (He buys a cat)
  • Wir haben das Auto. (We have the car)
  • Sie kennt keine Leute. (She knows no people)

👥 Possessiv Artikel (Possessive Articles)

What are Possessive Articles?
Possessive articles show ownership or relationship. They correspond to English "my, your, his, her, its, our, their" and change form based on the gender and case of the noun they modify.
🔑 Key Rule:
Possessive articles follow the same pattern as "ein"
• They change endings based on gender and case of the noun they modify
• The stem (mein-, dein-, sein-, etc.) stays the same, only endings change

• Remember: This follows the same pattern as ein → einen

🎯 Advanced Rules & Memory Tips

Formation Rules Summary
Understanding the systematic patterns behind German articles will help you remember them better.

🔵 Definite Articles (der/die/das)

  • Nominativ: der, die, das, die
  • Akkusativ: den, die, das, die
  • Rule: Only "der" changes to "den"
  • Memory: "Der Mann" → "Den Mann sehen"

🟡 Indefinite Articles (ein/eine)

  • Nominativ: ein, eine, ein, —
  • Akkusativ: einen, eine, ein, —
  • Rule: Only "ein" (masc) changes to "einen"
  • Memory: "Ein Mann" → "Einen Mann sehen"

❌ Negative Articles (kein/keine)

  • Nominativ: kein, keine, kein, keine
  • Akkusativ: keinen, keine, kein, keine
  • Rule: Follows "ein" pattern exactly
  • Memory: "Kein Mann" → "Keinen Mann sehen"

👥 Possessive Articles

  • Pattern: Follow "ein" endings exactly
  • Stems: mein-, dein-, sein-, ihr-, unser-, euer-
  • Akkusativ change: Only masculine adds -en
  • Memory: "Mein Mann" → "Meinen Mann sehen"
🧠 Master Memory Strategy:
The "Ein-Words" Family:
• ein, kein, mein, dein, sein, ihr, unser, euer ALL follow the same pattern
• Learn ONE pattern, apply to ALL possessive articles
• Masculine Akkusativ: Always add -en (einen, keinen, meinen, deinen, etc.)
• Everything else: Stays the same in Akkusativ
Common Mistakes to Avoid
❌ Wrong → ✅ Correct
❌ Ich sehe mein Vater → ✅ Ich sehe meinen Vater
❌ Er hat einen Schwester → ✅ Er hat eine Schwester
❌ Das ist meinen Auto → ✅ Das ist mein Auto
❌ Wir kaufen der Haus → ✅ Wir kaufen das Haus
Quick Reference Checklist
Before choosing an article, ask:
1. What gender is the noun? (der/die/das)
2. What case is needed? (Nominativ/Akkusativ)
3. What type of article? (definite/indefinite/possessive)
4. Is it masculine Akkusativ? If yes, use special forms (den/einen/meinen)
📚 Study Recommendations:
• Practice daily: Write 5 sentences using different possessive articles
• Focus on masculine: It's the only gender that changes in Akkusativ
• Use mnemonics: "Der Mann den ich kenne" (The man whom I know)
• Read aloud: Hearing the patterns helps memorization
